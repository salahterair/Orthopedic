Degenerative Joint Disease
(Osteoarthritis)

Year 4 MBBS · Orthopaedic Course

1

Definition & The Whole-Joint Concept

▼

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease characterised by breakdown of articular cartilage with proliferative (reactive) change in the surrounding and subchondral bone. It also involves the synovium, meniscus, tendons, ligaments, and periarticular muscle.

OA is a chronic musculoskeletal disease WITHOUT systemic involvement.

The old "wear-and-tear" label is outdated. OA is best understood as organ-level (whole-joint) failure — every tissue participates: hyaline cartilage, subchondral bone, synovium, capsule, ligaments and periarticular muscle. This is why OA produces pain, stiffness, instability and deformity together.
"Without systemic involvement" is the single line separating OA from Rheumatoid Arthritis in a viva. No fever, no weight loss, no anaemia of chronic disease, normal ESR/CRP → think degenerative, not inflammatory. When a patient with "OA" has systemic features, re-examine the diagnosis.

2

Epidemiology

▼
  • OA is the most common arthropathy and the leading cause of pain and disability in the Western world
  • Symptomatic knee OA affects ~15% of adults over 55
  • Radiographic OA is seen in >80% of people over 75
There is a large gap between radiographic and symptomatic disease — most people with X-ray OA are asymptomatic. Radiographic severity correlates only weakly with pain.

Why Pain Despite No Cartilage Nerves?

Cartilage is aneural and avascular. Pain in OA is driven by synovitis, subchondral bone changes (richly innervated), effusion and muscle weakness — not cartilage loss itself.

Never treat the X-ray — treat the patient. A patient with "bone-on-bone" films but little pain does not need a joint replacement. Conversely, modest radiographic change with severe functional loss may justify surgery.
3

Pathophysiology

▼

Pathogenesis = cartilage degradation + bone remodelling driven by an active response of chondrocytes and inflammatory cells. The balance tips toward catabolism.

Molecular Players

MoleculeTargetEffect
MMP-13 (collagenase-3)Type II collagenCleaves the main structural collagen of hyaline cartilage
ADAMTS-4 / -5 (aggrecanases)AggrecanDegrades major proteoglycan → loss of water binding → cartilage softens
IL-1β / TNF-αChondrocytesDrive MMP/ADAMTS production; suppress matrix synthesis

Loss of proteoglycan → loss of water → cartilage softens (chondromalacia), fibrillates, fissures, then delaminates.

The 4 Radiographic Hallmarks — "LOSS"

LOSS
L — Loss of joint space O — Osteophytes S — Subchondral cysts S — Subchondral Sclerosis
SignMechanism
Loss of joint spaceCartilage thinning
OsteophytesReactive fibrocartilage/bone outgrowths at joint margins
Subchondral sclerosisIncreased bone density where cartilage no longer cushions load (Wolff's law)
Subchondral cysts (geodes)Synovial fluid forced through microfractures in sclerotic bone, or focal necrosis
Subchondral bone (not cartilage) is richly innervated — sclerosis and cysts are a major source of the deep, aching OA pain. This is the rationale for load-offloading strategies: weight loss, bracing, osteotomy.

Risk Factors & Classification

▼

Primary OA (Idiopathic)

No obvious cause; age >40; direct correlation with ageing. Risk factors: advancing age, female sex, obesity, genetics, repetitive joint loading (manual-labour / daily micro-trauma).

Obesity acts via BOTH mechanical overload AND a metabolic/adipokine (leptin-driven) inflammatory effect — which is why obesity raises OA risk in non-weight-bearing joints such as the hands.

Female preponderance rises sharply after menopause, implicating oestrogen withdrawal.

Secondary OA — Known Causes

  • Trauma — intra-articular fracture, malunion
  • Congenital/Developmental — DDH (developmental dysplasia of the hip), Perthes, SUFE (slipped upper femoral epiphysis)
  • Infection — septic arthritis, brucella, TB
  • Inflammatory — Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Metabolic — Gout, CPPD (pseudogout)
  • Haematologic — Haemophilia (recurrent haemarthrosis)
  • Endocrine — Diabetes mellitus; also acromegaly, haemochromatosis
In a young patient (<40) with hip OA, actively hunt for a secondary cause — old DDH, Perthes, SUFE, prior fracture or avascular necrosis. "Primary OA in a 30-year-old hip" should make you distrust the label.

Symptoms & Signs

▼

Classic Symptom Complex

  • Mechanical pain — related to use, worsens through the day, relieved by rest
  • Morning stiffness <30 minutes
  • Gelling — transient stiffness after inactivity (eases within minutes of moving)
  • Reduced range of motion, joint instability, bony enlargement
  • Crepitus, variable effusion
  • Deformity restricting activities of daily living

Onset is insidious, chronic and gradually worsening. Pain worsens through the day; stiffness tends to improve as the day progresses.

"Gelling" = transient stiffness after sitting still (e.g. after a car journey or a film) that eases within minutes of moving — a very characteristic OA history point examiner likes to hear.

Joints Most Affected in OA

Small joints of hands and feet, hip, knee, spine (facet joints), first carpometacarpal (CMC) joint.

Heberden's & Bouchard's Nodes

  • Heberden's nodes = bony swelling at the DIP joints
  • Bouchard's nodes = bony swelling at the PIP joints
Memory hook: Heberden = Hard tips (DIP — "last" joint); Bouchard = Between (PIP — more proximal, "between" the tip and knuckle). These are osteophytes — hence bony, hard, and non-tender (unlike the soft, warm swelling of RA synovitis).
Nodal generalised OA (multiple Heberden/Bouchard nodes, often in post-menopausal women, familial) carries a good prognosis in the hand but predicts OA elsewhere, especially the knee.

OA of the Knee

▼

More common in obese females over 50.

Clinical Features

  • Mechanical pain; stiffness <30 min
  • Crepitus
  • Fixed flexion deformity — extension is lost first
  • Varus (bow-leg) malalignment — medial compartment predominance
  • Painful and restricted ROM; functional limitation

Why Varus? The Biomechanical Cycle

The medial compartment bears most of the knee load → medial-compartment OA predominates → progressive varus deformity → mechanical axis shifts further medial → increasing medial load → vicious cycle.

DeformityCompartmentTypical Context
Varus ("bow-leg")MedialPrimary OA (most common)
Valgus ("knock-knee")LateralInflammatory / RA knees
In a young varus knee with isolated medial OA, favour High Tibial Osteotomy (HTO) — offloads the medial compartment and preserves the native joint — over total knee replacement.
"Loss of extension first" → fixed flexion deformity. Document deformity, its correctability, and the mechanical axis before any surgical planning.

OA of the Hip

▼

More common in males over 40.

Clinical Features

  • Mechanical pain in the groin and gluteal region
  • Pain radiating to the medial knee (via obturator nerve)
  • Limited walking function and distance
  • Antalgic gait (limp)
  • Restricted and painful ROM
  • Leg-length discrepancy

Earliest ROM Loss

Internal rotation (and extension) are lost earliest in hip OA. Pain/loss on internal rotation is the most sensitive early sign — always test it. Reduced internal rotation is the first detectable sign before gross stiffness develops.
Hip pain refers to the medial knee via the obturator nerve — any patient with isolated knee pain must have the hip examined. "Examine the joint above and the joint below." Undiagnosed hip OA presenting as knee pain is a classic missed diagnosis.

True hip-joint pain is felt in the groin (the joint is anterior and deep). "Lateral hip pain" usually suggests trochanteric bursitis, not hip OA.

Hip OA: X-ray showing narrowed joint space and osteophyte formation

Differential Diagnosis

▼

General Differentials

  • Inflammatory arthropathy (Rheumatoid arthritis)
  • Crystal arthropathy — Gout, CPPD (pseudogout)
  • Septic arthritis
  • Fracture
  • Bursitis
  • Malignancy (primary or metastatic)

Joint-Specific Differentials

JointKey Differentials
HandDe Quervain's tenosynovitis, RA, Gout
HipTrochanteric bursitis, radiculopathy, spinal stenosis, iliotibial band syndrome
KneeMeniscal/ligament tear, chondromalacia patellae
Hot, acutely painful, swollen joint = Septic Arthritis until proven otherwise. Aspirate and send for Gram stain, culture, cell count and crystals before calling it an "OA flare". Missing a septic joint destroys cartilage within days — this is a medicolegal emergency.

Investigations

▼

OA is primarily a clinical diagnosis. Investigations mainly exclude differentials.

Blood Tests

  • FBC, ESR/CRP, urate — all normal in OA
  • Abnormal results → reconsider the diagnosis (inflammatory, infective, crystal disease)

Radiographs — The LOSS Mnemonic

LOSS
L — Loss of joint space O — Osteophytes S — Subchondral cysts S — Subchondral Sclerosis
Always request weight-bearing films for knee and hip OA — non-weight-bearing views underestimate true joint-space loss.

MRI

Not routine but detects early cartilage change, bone marrow oedema, and meniscal pathology.

Normal inflammatory markers + typical X-ray films + mechanical history = confident OA diagnosis. No further tests needed in most cases.

Management: Conservative (First-Line)

▼

Offered to ALL patients. Exhaust conservative options before moving to pharmacological or surgical intervention.

1
Education

Explain the condition, natural history, self-management strategies, and realistic expectations.

2
Exercise & Muscle Strengthening

Preferred activities: swimming, elliptical training, cycling (low-impact, load-sparing). Quadriceps strengthening reduces knee-OA pain independently of cartilage changes. Every 1 kg of weight loss reduces knee load by ~3–4 kg per step.

3
Weight Loss

The single most effective modifiable factor in lower-limb OA. Also reduces adipokine-driven systemic inflammation.

4
Physiotherapy

The most effective option for longer-term outcomes. Combines strengthening, proprioception and activity modification.

5
Joint Supports / Adjuncts

Local heat/ice; braces (unloader brace for medial knee OA); walking aids.

Management: Medical (Pharmacological)

▼

Add when conservative measures are insufficient. Follow the analgesic ladder.

AgentKey PointsSide Effects / Cautions
Paracetamol
(1st line)		 Max 4 g/day; efficacy comparable to NSAIDs with less toxicity; often first-line analgesic Hidden paracetamol in combination products → cumulative hepatotoxicity
Topical NSAIDs
(knee/hand OA)		 Preferred for knee and hand OA — efficacy with far less systemic toxicity Local skin reactions; minimal systemic absorption
Oral NSAIDs Use generic first; do NOT retard disease progression; lower doses may suffice GI ulcer/bleed, renal impairment, worsening CHF, oedema, CV risk. Add PPI for gastroprotection
COX-2 inhibitors Equivalent efficacy to non-selective NSAIDs; probably less GI toxicity Renal effects and oedema persist; screen for cardiovascular disease in older patients
Tramadol
(opioid reserve)		 Acts on opioid AND serotonin pathways; non-ulcerogenic; can add to NSAIDs/paracetamol. Reserve for refractory pain. Nausea, vomiting, lowered seizure threshold, constipation, drowsiness, dizziness

Intra-Articular Agents

AgentUseKey Points
Corticosteroid injection Acute flare / effusion Good short-term pain relief; mostly knee; no more often than every ~3 months; transient hyperglycaemia (caution in DM); infection risk
Hyaluronate (viscosupplementation) Knee only Requires a series of injections; modest symptomatic relief; no evidence of long-term structural benefit; expensive
Joint lavage Symptomatic benefit in selected patients Some demonstrated benefit; not widely used
Intra-articular injections buy time, not cure. Neither corticosteroid nor hyaluronate alters the natural history of OA. Counsel patients accordingly before the procedure.
13

Management: Surgical Options

▼

Only when conservative + medical measures fail and quality of life is substantially affected.

Three Named Procedures

ProcedureIndicationKey Points
Osteotomy
(e.g. High Tibial Osteotomy — HTO)		 Young, active patient; single-compartment (varus/medial) knee OA Realigns the mechanical axis to offload diseased compartment; preserves native joint; delays replacement
Arthroplasty
(Total Joint Replacement — TJR)		 Older patient; multi-compartment or advanced disease; disabling pain & functional loss Excellent outcomes for hip, knee, shoulder; modern prostheses last 15–20 years; most successful operations in medicine
Arthrodesis
(Joint Fusion)		 Salvage: young labourer, failed/infected arthroplasty, suitable small joints (ankle, wrist, 1st MTP) Sacrifices motion for durability; no further OA progression; indicated where function matters more than mobility
The main long-term issue with arthroplasty is aseptic loosening (wear-particle osteolysis), limiting prosthesis life — hence younger patients are steered toward osteotomy first. Peri-prosthetic infection is the feared early and late complication.

Total Hip Replacement (THR)
Acetabular cup and femoral stem components - lifespan 15-20 years
