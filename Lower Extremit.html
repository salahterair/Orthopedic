🗂️ Examination Framework ▼

⚠️
Joint Above & Below
🔑 Exam Principle
Always examine the joint above (hip) and below (ankle/foot) the site of pain. Hip pathology (e.g., slipped upper femoral epiphysis, avascular necrosis of the femoral head) frequently refers pain to the knee. Never attribute knee pain solely to local pathology without examining the hip.
🦴 Relevant Anatomy ▼
Anatomy

The Knee Joint Complex

The knee is the largest and most complex joint in the body. It comprises two articulations enclosed in a single capsule:

  • Tibiofemoral joint — the main weight-bearing articulation between the femoral condyles and tibial plateau. It is a modified hinge joint with a small degree of axial rotation.
  • Patellofemoral joint — the articulation between the patella and the patellar (trochlear) groove of the femur. Critical for efficient quadriceps force transmission; the patella increases the mechanical advantage of the quadriceps by up to 50%.

Ligamentous Stabilisers

Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL)
Prevents anterior tibial translation and internal rotation. Resists hyperextension.
Test: Lachman test (30° flexion), Anterior Drawer
Posterior Cruciate Ligament (PCL)
Prevents posterior tibial translation. Strongest knee ligament. Intact in 95% of dislocations alone.
Test: Posterior Drawer, Posterior Sag Sign
Medial Collateral Ligament (MCL)
Resists valgus (abduction) force and external rotation. Superficial and deep components.
Test: Valgus Stress Test at 30°
Lateral Collateral Ligament (LCL)
Resists varus (adduction) force. Part of the posterolateral corner complex.
Test: Varus Stress Test at 30°

The Menisci

The medial and lateral menisci are C-shaped fibrocartilaginous structures that deepen the tibial plateau, distribute load, provide shock absorption, and contribute to joint stability. The menisci bear approximately 50–70% of the compressive load through the knee. Key differences:

  • Medial meniscus — more C-shaped, attached to the MCL and joint capsule → less mobile → more commonly torn
  • Lateral meniscus — more circular, not attached to LCL → more mobile → less commonly torn, but torn more often in ACL injuries
  • The peripheral 25–30% of the meniscus is vascularised (red-red zone) and can heal; the inner avascular zone (white-white) cannot — this determines surgical strategy
📌 Screw-Home Mechanism
As the knee reaches full extension, the tibia externally rotates approximately 10° relative to the femur ("screwing home"), locking the joint for efficient weight bearing. This is reversed by the popliteus muscle ("unlocking" the knee) at the start of flexion. Loss of this mechanism suggests significant intra-articular pathology.

The Patellofemoral Joint and Q Angle

The Q angle is the angle between the line of pull of the quadriceps (ASIS to centre of patella) and the line from the centre of the patella to the tibial tuberosity. Normal: ≤15° in males, ≤20° in females. An increased Q angle increases the lateral vector on the patella, predisposing to patellar maltracking and lateral subluxation.

👁️ 1. Inspection ▼
Step 1

Expose both lower limbs fully from the thigh to the ankle. Inspect with the patient standing, walking, and lying supine. Compare both knees.

Skin and Soft Tissue

  • Erythema, warmth, swelling — the triad of a hot, red, swollen, and painful joint with systemic features (fever, malaise) mandates exclusion of septic arthritis (surgical emergency)
  • Surgical scars — arthroscopy portals (two small incisions anteromedially and anterolaterally), medial parapatellar incision (open surgery / arthroplasty), tibial tubercle scar (Osgood-Schlatter repair)
  • Bruising / ecchymosis — may track distally to the calf after a ligament rupture or haemarthrosis

Effusion Assessment

  • Mild effusion — loss of the peripatellar hollows (dimples medially and laterally to the patella)
  • Moderate effusion — visible suprapatellar bulge; ballottement of patella positive
  • Severe effusion — grossly distended joint; warm to touch; skin tense
🩺 Clinical Correlation — Haemarthrosis vs Effusion
Acute haemarthrosis (blood in the joint) develops within 2 hours of injury — characteristically a large, tense, warm effusion. In ~70% of cases, haemarthrosis indicates an ACL tear. Other causes: osteochondral fracture, peripheral meniscal tear (vascular zone), cruciate avulsion. A simple traumatic synovial effusion (straw-coloured fluid) develops over 6–24 hours and suggests meniscal tears, OA, or crystal arthropathy.

Bony and Alignment Deformities

  • Genu valgum — "knock knees"; medial compartment unloading, lateral compartment overload
  • Genu varum — "bow legs"; medial compartment overload → predisposes to medial OA
  • Genu recurvatum — hyperextension deformity; suggests ligamentous laxity or PCL insufficiency
  • Patellar displacement — lateral riding patella (patellar alta, trochlear dysplasia, increased Q angle)

Baker's Cyst

A popliteal (Baker's) cyst appears as a swelling in the posterior aspect of the knee in the popliteal fossa, medial to the midline. It represents distension of the gastrocnemius-semimembranosus bursa, which communicates with the joint in many adults. It is almost always secondary to intra-articular pathology (meniscal tear, OA, inflammatory arthritis). Rupture presents as sudden calf pain and swelling — mimicking a DVT (pseudothrombophlebitis).

Gait Analysis

  • Antalgic gait — shortened stance phase on the affected side due to pain with weight bearing; indicates any painful knee condition
  • Inability to bear weight — suggests significant structural injury (fracture, complete ligament rupture, locked meniscal tear)
  • Quadriceps avoidance gait — patient keeps the knee extended throughout stance to avoid quadriceps activation; seen in ACL-deficient knees
⚠️ Red Flag
A hot, red, monoarthritis in a child or adolescent who cannot bear weight is septic arthritis until proven otherwise. Urgent joint aspiration (cell count >50,000 WBC/mm³, >90% PMNs) and surgical washout are required. Do not delay for imaging. In adults with a replaced knee, any effusion with systemic features mandates aspiration.
🖐️ 2. Palpation ▼
Step 2

Palpate systematically with the patient supine, knee at rest in slight flexion (~20°). Identify the exact site of point tenderness to localise the pathology.

  1. Milking the effusion (bulge sign) — compress the suprapatellar pouch with your proximal hand, pushing fluid distally. Then stroke firmly up the medial side of the knee. Now tap the lateral side — a fluid wave (bulge) appearing on the medial side indicates a mild-to-moderate effusion. Significant effusions fill the medial and lateral peripatellar hollows.
  2. Patellar tap (ballottement) — compress the suprapatellar pouch with one hand to push fluid under the patella, then sharply tap (depress) the patella with two fingers. A click as the patella hits the femoral trochlea = moderate to large effusion.
  3. Patella mobility — palpate the patella directly, ensuring it moves freely medially and laterally. Reduced mobility suggests patellofemoral arthritis or adhesions. Crepitus under the patella with passive movement indicates patellofemoral arthritis.
  4. Peripatellar tenderness — palpate the medial and lateral facets of the patella, the inferior pole (patellar tendinopathy / Sinding-Larsen-Johansson disease), and the tibial tuberosity (Osgood-Schlatter disease in adolescents).
  5. Joint line tenderness — the most important palpation finding. With the knee flexed to 90°, place both thumbs along the medial and lateral joint lines (the concavities on either side of the patellar tendon). Press firmly. Medial joint line tenderness → medial meniscus or MCL; lateral joint line tenderness → lateral meniscus or LCL.
  6. Collateral ligaments — palpate the MCL along the medial joint (from femoral condyle to tibial flare) and the LCL from the lateral femoral condyle to the fibular head. Direct tenderness over the ligament substance suggests a sprain or tear.
  7. Popliteal fossa — palpate posteriorly with the knee slightly flexed. A fluctuant swelling medial to the midline = Baker's cyst. Popliteal artery pulsation is normally palpable; loss suggests vascular injury in knee dislocation.
🩺 Clinical Correlation — Joint Line Tenderness
Joint line tenderness (JLT) is the most sensitive clinical finding for meniscal tears (sensitivity ~86%). However, it is not specific — it is also present in collateral ligament injuries, OA, and capsular pathology. Medial JLT combined with a positive McMurray test increases diagnostic accuracy for medial meniscal tears significantly.

🔄 3. Range of Motion ▼
Step 3

Test active ROM first. Document all movements in degrees and always compare with the contralateral knee. Note pain at specific arc positions.

FlexionNormal: 135–140°
Full ExtensionNormal: 0° (straight)
HyperextensionNormal: up to 5°
Internal Rotation (at 90° flex)Normal: ~10°
External Rotation (at 90° flex)Normal: ~40°

Specific ROM Manoeuvres

Forced Flexion
Meniscal Compression Test

With the patient supine, maximally flex the knee by pressing the heel toward the buttock. Patients with a posterior horn meniscal tear cannot tolerate forced flexion — it compresses the posterior horns of the menisci between the femoral condyle and the tibial plateau. Pain in the posterolateral or posteromedial compartment = positive.

Extension Lag
Mechanical Block Assessment

Inability to fully extend the knee (extension lag) with the patient supine is clinically significant. Causes include: (1) locked knee — a displaced bucket-handle meniscal tear creates a mechanical block, typically limiting extension by 20–30°; (2) large haemarthrosis or effusion; (3) quadriceps weakness (extensor lag — passive extension normal but active extension limited).

Interpreting ROM Findings

ROM FindingLikely Diagnosis
Cannot tolerate forced flexionPosterior horn meniscal tear (posteromedial or posterolateral pain)
Limited extension (mechanical block ~20–30°)Locked knee — bucket-handle meniscal tear
Limited extension (pain-limited, no mechanical block)Haemarthrosis, large effusion, synovitis
Hyperextension beyond 5°PCL insufficiency, generalised ligamentous laxity, ACL tear (chronic)

🔑 Duck Walk Test
Ask the patient to walk while in a deep squat (fully flexed knees). If they are able to perform the duck walk without knee pain, a significant meniscal injury is unlikely. Inability to perform it or pain during the manoeuvre is consistent with meniscal pathology. Simple, quick, and useful as a screening test before more formal special tests.
⚖️ 4. Stability Tests ▼
Step 4

Stability tests assess the integrity of the four main knee ligaments. Always compare to the contralateral knee, especially in cases of generalised ligamentous laxity.

Lachman Test — ACL

Lachman Test
Anterior Cruciate Ligament
  1. Patient supine; knee flexed to 30°
  2. Stabilise the distal femur with one hand (grasp the outer/lateral aspect)
  3. With the other hand, grasp the proximal tibia and pull anteriorly
  4. Assess the degree of anterior tibial translation and quality of the endpoint
  5. Positive: increased anterior translation compared to the contralateral knee + soft or absent endpoint (mushy end-feel) = ACL tear
🎯 Exam Insight — Why Lachman Over Anterior Drawer?
At 30° flexion, the hamstrings are relaxed and cannot mask anterior translation. At 90° (anterior drawer position), the hamstrings wrap tightly around the knee and act as a secondary ACL, producing false-negative results — especially in acute ACL tears with haemarthrosis. The Lachman test has sensitivity ~85%, specificity ~94% for complete ACL tears.

Posterior Drawer Test & Posterior Sag Sign — PCL

Posterior Drawer
Posterior Cruciate Ligament
  1. Patient supine; hip at 45°, knee at 90°, foot flat on the table
  2. Sit on the patient's foot to stabilise it
  3. Grasp the proximal tibia with both thumbs on the joint line anteriorly
  4. Push the tibia posteriorly with both hands
  5. Positive: increased posterior translation of the tibia relative to the femur = PCL tear
📌 Posterior Sag Sign (Godfrey's Test)
With both hips and knees flexed to 90° while the patient is supine, the tibia of the PCL-deficient knee sags posteriorly under gravity compared to the normal side. This is highly specific for PCL injury and should be checked before the posterior drawer to avoid being falsely negative from a reduced starting position.
🩺 Clinical Correlation — PCL Injury Mechanism
PCL tears typically result from a dashboard injury (posterior force on the proximal tibia with a flexed knee in a road traffic accident) or a direct blow to the anterior tibia in sports. The PCL is the strongest knee ligament and isolated PCL tears are less common than ACL tears — they are often part of a multi-ligament injury in knee dislocation.

Varus & Valgus Stress Tests — LCL & MCL

Valgus Stress
Medial Collateral Ligament (MCL)
  1. Patient supine, leg resting with the heel on the table
  2. Flex the knee to 30° (isolates the MCL)
  3. One hand stabilises the lateral aspect of the thigh; the other holds the medial ankle
  4. Apply an abduction (valgus) force — the medial compartment of the knee is forced open
  5. Positive: medial joint pain or gapping = MCL tear
Varus Stress
Lateral Collateral Ligament (LCL)
  1. Same position; knee flexed to 30°
  2. Apply an adduction (varus) force — the lateral compartment is opened
  3. Positive: lateral joint pain or gapping = LCL tear
🎯 Exam Insight — Test at 0° and 30°
Always test collateral ligaments at both 30° flexion (collateral ligaments most isolated) and 0° (full extension). Laxity only at 30° = isolated collateral ligament injury. Laxity at both 30° AND 0° = more extensive injury involving the posterior capsule, cruciate ligaments, and possibly the posterolateral corner (grade III multi-ligament injury).

McMurray Test — Meniscal Injury

McMurray Test
Medial & Lateral Meniscus
  1. Patient supine; flex the knee fully to 90° or beyond
  2. Medial meniscus: place one hand on the lateral joint line, grasp the foot, apply valgus stress + external rotation, then slowly extend the knee
  3. Positive for medial meniscus: pain or a palpable/audible click at the medial joint line
  4. Lateral meniscus: stabilise the medial knee, apply varus stress + internal rotation, then slowly extend
  5. Positive for lateral meniscus: pain or click at the lateral joint line
🩺 Clinical Correlation — Meniscal Tears
Meniscal tears are among the most common sports injuries. Acute tears in young patients typically result from a twisting injury; degenerative tears in older patients occur from minimal trauma. The posterior horn of the medial meniscus is the most commonly injured part. MRI has replaced diagnostic arthroscopy — it has ~90% sensitivity and specificity for meniscal tears. The McMurray test has moderate sensitivity (~53%) but higher specificity (~59%).

🔬 5. Special Tests ▼
Step 5

Special tests are performed to confirm specific diagnoses following the standard examination. Focus particularly on the patellofemoral joint and remaining meniscal assessment.

Patellofemoral Joint Tests

Patellar Apprehension Test

Tests for: Patellar instability / recurrent lateral patellar dislocation

  1. Patient supine with the knee relaxed in 20–30° flexion (the position of maximum patellar instability)
  2. Gently push the patella laterally with both thumbs while the quadriceps are relaxed
  3. Positive: the patient braces, contracts the quadriceps, or grabs the examiner's hand — apprehension that the patella is about to dislocate
  4. Pain alone is less specific; true apprehension (protective contraction) is the meaningful endpoint
🎯 Exam Insight
A positive apprehension test is pathognomonic of previous lateral patellar dislocation or significant patellar instability. Lateral patellar dislocation is most common in adolescent females and is associated with an increased Q angle, trochlear dysplasia, and patella alta. The medial patellofemoral ligament (MPFL) is torn in virtually all first-time dislocations.
Patellar Grind Test
Patellofemoral Syndrome / Chondromalacia Patellae
  1. Patient supine with knee fully extended and quadriceps relaxed
  2. Apply a posteriorly-directed force to the patella (compress it against the femoral trochlea)
  3. Ask the patient to actively contract the quadriceps (straight leg raise)
  4. Positive: pain or crepitus on quadriceps contraction = patellofemoral joint pathology (chondromalacia, OA)

Apley's Compression Test

Apley's Test
Meniscal Compression

Tests for: Meniscal injury (compression component) vs ligament injury (distraction component)

  1. Patient prone, knee flexed to 90°
  2. Stabilise the posterior thigh with your knee (or one hand)
  3. Compression phase: apply a downward force through the foot (axial compression) while rotating the tibia internally and externally
  4. Positive compression: pain reproduced = meniscal injury
  5. Distraction phase: lift the foot upward (axial distraction) while rotating — positive distraction = ligamentous injury (collateral or capsular)
📌 Memory Aid — Apley's Grind vs Distraction
Compress → Meniscus hurts (menisci are compressed between femur and tibia)
Distract → Ligament hurts (ligaments are stressed under tension when joint is pulled apart)

🩺 Clinical Correlation — O'Donoghue's Unhappy Triad
A valgus force combined with external rotation (e.g., lateral tackle in football) can simultaneously injure the ACL + MCL + meniscus. This was classically described as the medial meniscus; modern evidence shows the lateral meniscus is more often injured alongside ACL tears (because the lateral compartment is distracted and then compressed as the knee rebounds). Always assess all three structures when one ligament is torn.
