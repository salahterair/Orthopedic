Septic Arthritis

Infective / Suppurative Arthritis · Year 4 MBBS · Orthopaedic Course

🚨 ORTHOPAEDIC EMERGENCY  —  A hot, swollen, painful joint is septic until proven otherwise. Aspirate first, treat without delay.
1

Definition

▼

Septic arthritis is inflammation of the synovial membrane with a purulent (pus-containing) effusion into the joint capsule, most often due to bacterial infection.

Why the Synovium?

The synovium is the vascular lining of the joint — blood-borne bacteria seed it first during bacteraemia. Once infection is intra-articular, the joint becomes a closed abscess cavity that pus cannot easily escape → rising pressure and rapid, irreversible cartilage damage.

Purulent effusion is the key phrase. The diagnosis is confirmed by aspirating frank pus or grossly turbid fluid — not by imaging. If clinical suspicion is high, aspirate regardless of X-ray findings.

Clinical photo: hot, erythematous, swollen knee in septic arthritis

2

Etiology — Organisms by Host

▼
  • Commonest organism overall: Staphylococcus aureus
  • ~80% gram-positive aerobes; ~20% gram-negative
  • 50% of cases occur in children <5 years; 30% in children <2 years
  • Haemophilus influenzae incidence has fallen sharply since the Hib vaccine

Why the Hip in Infants?

The infant hip has an intracapsular metaphysis with a rich metaphyseal blood supply, making haematogenous seeding — and spread from adjacent osteomyelitis — particularly common. The hip is also a deep, closed, tense compartment.

A septic hip in a child is a surgical emergency. The deep, tense, closed compartment means rising pressure can cause avascular necrosis (AVN) of the femoral head within hours. Do not delay for imaging — proceed to urgent washout.
4

Sources of Infection — Routes of Entry

▼
  1. Haematogenous — bacteraemia (most common overall); IV drug injection; distant focus (skin, urine, dental)
  2. Direct trauma / penetrating injury — or iatrogenic (joint injection, arthroscopy, joint replacement surgery)
  3. Spread from adjacent osteomyelitis — bone infection breaking into the joint
  4. Contiguous soft-tissue infection — cellulitis, abscess, bursitis, tenosynovitis spreading inward
The anatomical rule — joints at risk from osteomyelitis: In joints where the metaphysis lies inside the joint capsule — Hip, Shoulder, Elbow, Ankle — metaphyseal osteomyelitis can rupture directly into the joint. When in doubt, assume BOTH osteomyelitis AND septic arthritis are present.
Always search for a primary focus — skin abscess, recent injection, IV line, dental procedure, urinary source — because eradicating it prevents relapse.
5

Pathogenesis — The Destructive Cascade

▼

The synovium is highly vascular with no limiting basement-membrane barrier, so bacteria seed it easily during bacteraemia.

Bacterial invasion of the synovium → phagocytosis by fibroblast-like synoviocytes
Influx of inflammatory cells (predominantly neutrophils)
Release of proteolytic enzymes (bacterial toxins + neutrophil-derived proteases)
Synovial necrosis + articular surface destruction
Accumulation of purulent synovial fluid → pressure necrosis
Irreversible cartilage and bone destruction
Why cartilage dies so fast: Enzymes are from BOTH bacteria (toxins) AND the host's own neutrophils. Neutrophil-derived proteases begin degrading glycosaminoglycans within 8 hours and collagen within days — damage is irreversible and largely host-mediated. This is the pathological basis of "treat as an emergency."
Because damage is enzyme-driven and pressure-driven, draining the pus is as important as antibiotics — you must decompress the joint and remove the enzyme-laden effusion. Antibiotics alone are insufficient if the joint remains undrained.

In Infants

The epiphysis is cartilaginous in infants and is destroyed directly. Untreated infection spreads to underlying bone → forms an abscess or sinus → enters the bloodstream → septicaemia.

6

Clinical Presentation

▼

Classic Presentation

  • Fever
  • Erythema, oedema, heat over the joint
  • Severe pain
  • Markedly decreased active AND passive range of motion (the joint is held still — "pseudoparalysis")
Pain on passive movement is the key clinical sign. In inflammatory/mechanical conditions a patient may tolerate gentle passive motion; in septic arthritis even tiny passive movement is agonising because it raises intra-articular pressure on an inflamed capsule. Refusal of ALL movement is a red flag.

Age-Related Presentations

Patient GroupPresentation FeaturesLikely Organism
Young, sexually active adult Fever + tenosynovitis + migratory polyarthralgia + dermatitis (papular/haemorrhagic rash on trunk + extensor surfaces of distal limbs) — the DGI triad N. gonorrhoeae
IV drug user Unusual joints (SI joint, sternoclavicular); fever + joint pain Pseudomonas
Infant / non-verbal child Fever + irritability + refuses to move limb or bear weight (pseudoparalysis); local signs may be absent in deep joints S. aureus
In a non-verbal child, refusal to move or bear weight + fever = septic joint until excluded. Do not be reassured by absent local signs in a deep joint like the hip — internal joint pressure may be the only abnormality detectable clinically.

Kocher criteria guide but do NOT replace aspiration. A child meeting 3–4 criteria should proceed to urgent hip aspiration/washout. A child with 0–1 criteria and a well appearance is more likely transient synovitis (all investigations normal, resolves spontaneously in 2 weeks).
Transient synovitis vs Septic arthritis: Transient synovitis is self-limiting, affects children 3–10 years, follows a viral URTI, all investigations are normal. Any deviation from "all normal" should raise suspicion for septic arthritis.
8

Investigations

▼
Definitive diagnosis = Joint Aspiration (Arthrocentesis) BEFORE antibiotics. Starting antibiotics before aspiration suppresses bacterial growth, potentially rendering cultures falsely negative and delaying diagnosis.

Joint Aspiration — What to Send

  • Gram stain
  • Culture & Sensitivity (C/S)
  • WBC count with differential
  • Crystal analysis (to exclude gout / pseudogout)

Aspirate directly for accessible joints (knee, elbow); use ultrasound or X-ray guidance for deep/difficult joints (hip, sacroiliac, sternoclavicular).

Synovial Fluid WBC Interpretation

CategoryWBC CountPredominant Cell
Normal<200/µLMononuclear
Non-inflammatory (OA)<2,000/µLMixed
Inflammatory (RA / Gout)2,000–50,000/µLNeutrophils
SEPTIC ARTHRITIS>50,000/µL (often >100,000)>75–90% Neutrophils (PMN)
A WBC <50,000/µL does not exclude sepsis — especially with gonococcal infection, partially treated disease, or prosthetic joints. Culture is definitive; never rule out infection on cell count alone.

Blood / Lab Studies

  • FBC — leukocytosis with left shift
  • ESR + CRP — elevated; used to monitor response to treatment (CRP falls faster than ESR)
  • Blood cultures — positive in ~50% of S. aureus septic arthritis
  • Urethral / cervical / pharyngeal / rectal swabs — if gonococcus suspected

Detail

  • Dislocation — tense effusion (especially infant hip)
  • AVN of the femoral head — in children; caused by pressure-induced ischaemia of the intracapsular blood supply
  • Epiphyseal destruction — cartilaginous epiphysis in infants is directly destroyed → unstable pseudarthrosis
  • Physeal damage → growth arrest → limb shortening or angular deformity
  • Ankylosis — eroded cartilage heals with bony or fibrous fusion → stiff joint
  • Secondary OA — post-infective cartilage loss accelerates degenerative change
Every hour of delay adds irreversible damage. In children the threats are AVN, growth arrest and limb-length discrepancy; in adults, cartilage loss → secondary OA and stiffness. The urgency is absolute.
11

Treatment — Emergency Management

▼
Step 1: Aspirate the joint and examine the fluid. Step 2: Take blood cultures. Step 3: Start IV empirical antibiotics immediately after sampling — NEVER before.

11A. General Measures

1
Joint Aspiration — immediately

Decompresses the joint, removes enzyme-laden pus, provides diagnostic material. Repeat daily if needle aspiration is the chosen drainage strategy.

2
Analgesia + Splinting

Splint in position of maximal comfort. Adequate analgesia while awaiting surgical drainage.

3
IV fluid resuscitation + nutritional support

Treat systemic sepsis aggressively.

4
Identify and treat all foci of infection

Hunt the primary source (skin, IV line, urine, dental) — eradicating it prevents relapse.

11B. Antibiotics — Empirical Choice

Gram-positive confirmed

Flucloxacillin
(covers S. aureus)

Unknown / gram-negative risk

3rd-generation cephalosporin
(covers G+ and G−)

Child <4 yr — suspect H. influenzae

Ampicillin

Young sexually active — gonococcus

Ceftriaxone

MRSA risk (hospital, IVDU)

Add Vancomycin

IVDU — Pseudomonas

Antipseudomonal agent
(e.g. piperacillin-tazobactam)

Duration: IV antibiotics for 1–2 weeks, then switch to oral antibiotics for ~6 weeks total. Always switch to targeted therapy once sensitivities are known. Monitor response with CRP and ESR trends — CRP falls first.

11C. Surgical Drainage — Indications

  1. Joints not responding to antibiotics + daily arthrocentesis
  2. Joints with limited accessibility — hip (especially in children), sternoclavicular joint
  3. Patients with underlying comorbidity — diabetes, RA, immunosuppression — treat more aggressively with earlier surgery
"Septic hip = open washout." The hip in children and adults requires surgical arthrotomy and washout as an emergency. It is too deep for reliable repeated needle aspiration and the consequences of delayed drainage (AVN, growth arrest) are catastrophic.

Enhancement: Prosthetic Joint Infection (PJI)

  • Organisms form a biofilm on the implant that antibiotics cannot penetrate
  • Early (<3 months): S. aureus; Late/indolent: coagulase-negative Staph (S. epidermidis)
  • Diagnosis: aspiration + culture (prolonged incubation needed) + elevated CRP/ESR
  • Cure usually requires removal of the implant (single- or two-stage revision) + prolonged antibiotics — antibiotics alone rarely eradicate biofilm

✓ Correct: B — Pseudomonas; CT or MRIIV drug users are at particular risk of Pseudomonas aeruginosa septic arthritis, which characteristically affects axial joints — sacroiliac joint and sternoclavicular joint — not the peripheral joints commonly affected in other patients. CT is preferred for the sternoclavicular joint; MRI is best for periarticular osteomyelitis and sacroiliac involvement. Aspiration of the SI joint requires CT or fluoroscopic guidance.
