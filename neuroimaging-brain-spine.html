Neuroimaging of the Brain and Spine

Reading Material for Fourth-Year Medical Students

Introduction to Neuroimaging

▸

Neuroimaging plays a central role in the diagnosis and management of neurological disorders. Appropriate selection of the imaging modality depends on the clinical presentation, suspected pathology, and urgency of the condition. Computed tomography (CT) is the primary imaging modality in most neurological emergencies because of its speed and availability, whereas magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) provides superior soft tissue contrast and greater sensitivity for many intracranial and spinal disorders.

Common Clinical Indications

  • Head trauma
  • Acute focal neurological deficit (suspected stroke or transient ischaemic attack)
  • Sudden severe headache or suspected subarachnoid haemorrhage
  • Raised intracranial pressure
  • Suspected central nervous system infection
  • New-onset seizures
  • Persistent or progressive focal neurological deficits
  • Congenital abnormalities and follow-up of known intracranial lesions

Imaging Modalities

▸
ModalityPrincipal Clinical Applications
CT Head (Non-contrast)First-line investigation in neurological emergencies. Acute stroke (to exclude intracranial haemorrhage before reperfusion therapy); head trauma; acute intracranial haemorrhage; sudden severe headache (suspected subarachnoid haemorrhage).
CT Angiography (CTA)Evaluation of intracranial and extracranial vessels. Common indications include suspected large vessel occlusion, cerebral aneurysm, arterial dissection, and vascular malformations.

MRI SpineEvaluation of spinal cord compression, cauda equina syndrome, disc disease, spinal infection, inflammatory disorders, trauma, and spinal tumours.

Relevant Neuroanatomy

▸

Accurate interpretation of neuroimaging requires recognition of the major cerebral lobes, ventricular system, deep grey nuclei, brainstem, cerebellum, and principal vascular territories. These anatomical structures provide the basis for localization of neurological disease and interpretation of common imaging abnormalities.

  • Frontal Lobe: Executive function, behaviour, voluntary motor function, and expressive language (Broca's area).
  • Parietal Lobe: Somatic sensation, spatial awareness, and sensory integration.
  • Temporal Lobe: Auditory processing, language comprehension (Wernicke's area), and memory (hippocampus).
  • Occipital Lobe: Visual processing.
  • Cerebellum: Coordination of movement, balance, and posture.
  • Brainstem: Midbrain, pons, and medulla; contains cranial nerve nuclei and vital autonomic centres.
  • Basal Ganglia: Important in motor control and a common site of hypertensive intracerebral haemorrhage and lacunar infarction.
  • Internal Capsule: Contains major ascending and descending white matter tracts; small infarcts may produce profound contralateral motor deficits.
  • Thalamus: Principal sensory relay nucleus and another common site of hypertensive haemorrhage.

Cerebral Vascular Territories

Understanding vascular territories is essential for localization of ischaemic stroke.

  • Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA): Medial frontal and parietal lobes. Infarction typically causes contralateral lower-limb weakness and sensory loss.
  • Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA): Lateral cerebral hemisphere. Infarction commonly produces contralateral face and upper-limb weakness, aphasia (dominant hemisphere), or hemispatial neglect (non-dominant hemisphere). This is the most frequently affected vascular territory in acute ischaemic stroke.
  • Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA): Occipital lobe and inferior temporal lobe. Infarction commonly results in contralateral homonymous visual field defects.

Common Neurological Disorders

▸

1. Acute Ischaemic Stroke

Definition

Acute ischaemic stroke results from occlusion of a cerebral artery, leading to reduced cerebral perfusion and irreversible infarction if blood flow is not restored promptly.

Initial Imaging

Non-contrast CT of the head is the first-line imaging investigation because it rapidly excludes intracranial haemorrhage before reperfusion therapy is considered.

Key Imaging Findings

  • Early Non-contrast CT — may be normal during the first few hours after symptom onset. Early findings include loss of grey–white matter differentiation, sulcal effacement, and the hyperdense middle cerebral artery (MCA) sign.
  • MRI (Diffusion-Weighted Imaging) — DWI demonstrates restricted diffusion within minutes of infarction. Acute infarction appears hyperintense on DWI and hypointense on the apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) map, representing true restricted diffusion.
Clinical CorrelationA normal CT scan does not exclude acute ischaemic stroke.

2. Intracranial Haemorrhage

A. Intracerebral Haemorrhage — Definition

Intracerebral haemorrhage results from rupture of a cerebral blood vessel, leading to bleeding within the brain parenchyma. Hypertension is the most common cause of spontaneous intracerebral haemorrhage, although vascular malformations, cerebral amyloid angiopathy, anticoagulation, and intracranial tumours should also be considered.

Imaging Findings

  • Non-contrast CT is the investigation of choice in the acute setting.
  • Acute haemorrhage appears as a hyperdense (bright white) intraparenchymal lesion.
  • Common sites: basal ganglia (most common), thalamus, pons, cerebellum.
  • Secondary findings: surrounding vasogenic oedema, mass effect, midline shift, ventricular compression, hydrocephalus, and brain herniation.

C. Subarachnoid Haemorrhage — Definition

Subarachnoid haemorrhage is bleeding into the subarachnoid space surrounding the brain. The most common non-traumatic cause is rupture of a saccular (berry) aneurysm.

Clinical Presentation

Sudden severe ("thunderclap") headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, vomiting, reduced level of consciousness, and meningism.

Imaging Findings

  • Non-contrast CT demonstrates hyperdense blood within the basal cisterns, sylvian fissures, and cerebral sulci.
  • CT sensitivity is highest during the first few hours after symptom onset.
  • If CT is negative but clinical suspicion remains high, lumbar puncture may demonstrate xanthochromia, depending on timing and local protocols.
  • CT angiography (CTA) or MR angiography (MRA) is performed to identify the underlying aneurysm or other vascular abnormality.

Brain Tumours

▸

Brain tumours are broadly classified as extra-axial (arising outside the brain parenchyma) or intra-axial (arising within the brain).

Meningioma

  • Usually a benign extra-axial tumour.
  • Well-circumscribed with a broad dural attachment.
  • Homogeneous contrast enhancement.
  • A dural tail is a characteristic imaging feature, although it is not pathognomonic.
  • Adjacent hyperostosis may be present.

Glioblastoma

Glioblastoma is the most common primary malignant brain tumour in adults. Typical MRI features include irregular infiltrative margins, thick irregular ring enhancement, central necrosis, extensive surrounding vasogenic oedema, and significant mass effect.

Clinical CorrelationRing enhancement is not specific for glioblastoma. Similar appearances occur in cerebral abscess, metastatic disease, tuberculoma, toxoplasmosis, and tumefactive demyelinating lesions. Clinical history and imaging characteristics should always be interpreted together.

Central Nervous System Infections

▸

Herpes Simplex Encephalitis

Herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) is the most common cause of sporadic fatal viral encephalitis.

Clinical Features

Fever, altered mental status, seizures, behavioural changes, and focal neurological deficits.

MRI Findings

  • Hyperintense signal on T2-weighted and FLAIR sequences.
  • Predominantly involves the temporal lobes.
  • Early disease may be unilateral or asymmetrical before progressing to bilateral involvement.
  • Diffusion restriction and contrast enhancement may also be present.

Cerebral Tuberculoma

Tuberculoma is a localized granulomatous lesion caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis.

Imaging Findings

  • Solitary or multiple ring-enhancing lesions.
  • Frequently located within the supratentorial compartment.
  • Variable surrounding vasogenic oedema.
  • May mimic metastases, cerebral abscesses, or high-grade gliomas.
Clinical CorrelationClinical history and microbiological investigations are essential for diagnosis.

Hydrocephalus and Cerebral Atrophy

▸

Enlargement of the ventricular system may result from hydrocephalus or cerebral atrophy. Differentiating these conditions is essential because hydrocephalus may require urgent neurosurgical intervention, whereas cerebral atrophy generally reflects chronic loss of brain volume.

Hydrocephalus

Definition: Abnormal enlargement of the ventricular system due to impaired cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) circulation or absorption.

Imaging Findings

  • Enlargement of the lateral, third, and/or fourth ventricles.
  • Narrow or effaced cortical sulci despite ventricular enlargement.
  • Periventricular low attenuation on CT or high T2/FLAIR signal on MRI due to transependymal CSF flow.
  • Depending on the cause, an obstructing lesion may be identified.

Cerebral Atrophy

Definition: Loss of brain parenchymal volume resulting in compensatory enlargement of the ventricles and cortical sulci.

Imaging Findings

  • Enlarged ventricles.
  • Prominent cortical sulci.
  • Widened subarachnoid spaces.
  • No evidence of transependymal CSF flow.
Exam InsightHydrocephalus: enlarged ventricles with compressed cortical sulci. Cerebral atrophy: enlarged ventricles with prominent cortical sulci.

Spinal Disorders

▸

MRI is the imaging modality of choice for most spinal disorders because it provides excellent visualization of the spinal cord, intervertebral discs, nerve roots, ligaments, and surrounding soft tissues.

Lumbar Disc Herniation

Definition: Occurs when degeneration or rupture of the annulus fibrosus allows displacement of nucleus pulposus material, resulting in compression of adjacent nerve roots.

Imaging Findings

  • Reduced disc height.
  • Posterior disc protrusion or extrusion.
  • Compression of the thecal sac and/or exiting nerve root.
  • Associated spinal canal or foraminal stenosis may be present.

Spondylolisthesis

Definition: Anterior displacement of one vertebral body relative to the vertebra below it, most commonly involving L5 on S1.

Imaging Findings

  • Anterior vertebral displacement.
  • Step-off deformity on sagittal imaging.
  • Associated degenerative disc disease or pars interarticularis defects may be present.

Syringomyelia

Definition: The presence of a fluid-filled cavity (syrinx) within the spinal cord.

Common Associations

Chiari type I malformation, spinal cord trauma, intramedullary tumours, and previous meningitis.

MRI Findings

  • Well-defined intramedullary cavity with cerebrospinal fluid signal intensity.
  • Variable cord expansion depending on lesion size.

Cauda Equina Syndrome

Definition: A neurosurgical emergency caused by compression of the lumbosacral nerve roots below the conus medullaris.

Clinical Features

  • Severe low back pain.
  • Bilateral lower limb weakness or sensory disturbance.
  • Saddle anaesthesia.
  • Bladder dysfunction or urinary retention.
  • Bowel dysfunction.
  • Reduced anal sphincter tone.
Clinical CorrelationMRI of the lumbar spine is the investigation of choice and should be performed urgently when cauda equina syndrome is suspected.

Fundamental MRI Sequences

▸

Magnetic resonance imaging consists of multiple pulse sequences, each designed to highlight specific anatomical structures or pathological processes. Understanding the basic characteristics of these sequences facilitates interpretation of radiology reports and correlation with clinical findings.

SequenceImaging CharacteristicsClinical Applications
T1-weightedFat appears bright and water appears dark. Excellent anatomical detail.Normal anatomy, fat-containing structures, subacute haemorrhage, and post-contrast enhancement. Enhancement reflects disruption of the blood–brain barrier (BBB).
T2-weightedWater and fluid appear bright.Demonstrates oedema, inflammation, tumours, infection, infarction, and most pathological processes of the brain and spinal cord.
FLAIRT2-weighted sequence with suppression of CSF signal.Improves detection of periventricular and cortical lesions, including MS plaques, cerebral oedema, encephalitis, and subarachnoid pathology.
DWI / ADCEvaluates diffusion of water molecules within tissues.Acute infarction: hyperintense on DWI with low ADC signal (true restricted diffusion). Also valuable in cerebral abscess and Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease.
GRE / SWIHighly sensitive to magnetic susceptibility from blood products and mineralization.Detection of haemorrhage, cerebral microbleeds, diffuse axonal injury, cavernous malformations, and haemorrhagic transformation. Calcification may appear similar — correlate with CT.

Standard Imaging Planes

  • Axial (Transverse): Horizontal sections dividing the body into superior and inferior portions. Widely used for intracranial pathology.
  • Sagittal: Vertical sections dividing right from left. Useful for the corpus callosum, pituitary gland, brainstem, spinal cord, and vertebral alignment.
  • Coronal: Vertical sections dividing anterior from posterior. Useful for the temporal lobes, hippocampi, pituitary region, paranasal sinuses, skull base, and ventricular anatomy.

Chapter Summary

▸
  • Non-contrast CT is the first-line investigation in most acute neurological emergencies because it rapidly identifies intracranial haemorrhage and major structural abnormalities.
  • MRI provides superior soft tissue contrast and is more sensitive than CT for acute ischaemic infarction, inflammatory disorders, neoplasms, posterior fossa lesions, and spinal pathology.
  • Interpretation requires integration of the clinical presentation, anatomical localization, and characteristic imaging findings.
  • Recognition of common vascular territories facilitates localization of acute stroke.
  • Differentiation of intra-axial and extra-axial lesions assists in formulating a differential diagnosis.
  • Knowledge of the basic MRI sequences (T1, T2, FLAIR, DWI/ADC, GRE/SWI) enables accurate interpretation of radiology reports.

Suggested Self-Directed Image Review

When reviewing neuroimaging, systematically identify: (1) the imaging modality (CT or MRI); (2) the imaging plane (axial, sagittal, or coronal); (3) the MRI sequence, if applicable; (4) the anatomical structures demonstrated; (5) the principal abnormality; (6) the likely diagnosis; and (7) the associated clinical significance. This systematic approach promotes accurate interpretation and strengthens clinicoradiological correlation.
