⚠️
Joint Above & Below
🔑 Exam Principle
Always examine the joint above (cervical spine / acromioclavicular joint) and below (elbow) the site of pain. Referred pain from C5–C6 radiculopathy commonly presents as shoulder pain.
🦴 Relevant Anatomy ▼
Anatomy

The Shoulder Girdle

The shoulder complex comprises four articulations that work in concert:

  • Glenohumeral (GH) joint — ball-and-socket joint between the humeral head and the glenoid fossa; most mobile joint in the body, stabilised primarily by dynamic (rotator cuff) and static (labrum, capsule, ligaments) stabilisers
  • Acromioclavicular (AC) joint — plane synovial joint between the acromion and lateral clavicle; stabilised by the AC ligament and coracoclavicular (CC) ligaments (conoid and trapezoid)
  • Sternoclavicular (SC) joint — only true bony articulation of the upper extremity with the axial skeleton
  • Scapulothoracic articulation — a physiological joint; contributes approximately 60° of the 180° of total shoulder abduction (scapulohumeral rhythm: 2:1 ratio of GH to scapulothoracic motion)
🔬 Rotator Cuff (SITS)
Supraspinatus (abduction, 0–15°) · Infraspinatus (external rotation) · Teres minor (external rotation) · Subscapularis (internal rotation). All insert on the greater or lesser tuberosity of the humerus and dynamically compress the humeral head into the glenoid.

Subacromial Space

The subacromial space lies between the superior humeral head/greater tuberosity and the undersurface of the acromion and coracoacromial arch. It contains the supraspinatus tendon, subacromial bursa, and the long head of biceps tendon. Narrowing of this space (e.g., type III hooked acromion, acromial osteophytes) leads to impingement syndrome.

👁️ 1. Inspection ▼
Step 1

Expose both shoulders fully. Inspect from anterior, posterior, and lateral aspects. Compare both sides.

What to Look For

Skin and Soft Tissue

  • Erythema, swelling, warmth — think septic arthritis (surgical emergency), crystal arthropathy (gout, CPPD), or acute bursitis
  • Surgical scars — previous arthroscopy portals (3 small puncture marks), open rotator cuff repair, arthroplasty
  • Bruising — may indicate recent fracture (clavicle, proximal humerus) or rotator cuff avulsion

Bony Contour

  • Squared-off / flattened deltoid contour — pathognomonic of anterior glenohumeral dislocation; loss of the normal rounded deltoid outline with a prominent coracoid process anteriorly
  • Step deformity at AC joint — Grade III+ AC joint separation; the distal clavicle rides superiorly relative to the acromion
  • Prominent SC joint — SC joint dislocation (posterior dislocation is a vascular emergency)
  • Winging of scapula — paralysis of serratus anterior (long thoracic nerve injury) causing medial border of scapula to protrude posteriorly; ask patient to do a wall push-up

Muscle

  • Deltoid wasting — axillary nerve palsy (post-dislocation), C5 radiculopathy
  • Supraspinatus / infraspinatus fossa wasting — chronic rotator cuff tear, suprascapular nerve entrapment
  • Popeye deformity — distal migration of the biceps muscle belly due to rupture of the long head of biceps tendon (appears as a bulge in mid-arm)
🩺 Clinical Correlation
Anterior shoulder dislocation is the most common type (95%). It occurs when the arm is abducted and externally rotated. The inferior capsule is stretched and the humeral head dislocates anteroinferiorly, resulting in the classic squared-off appearance. Always check axillary nerve function (sensation over regimental badge area) before and after reduction.
⚠️ Red Flag
Signs of infection (erythema, warmth, swelling, restricted ROM in all planes) in the shoulder mandate urgent assessment for septic arthritis. Joint aspiration for cell count, Gram stain, and culture is required. Do not delay surgical washout.
🖐️ 2. Palpation ▼
Step 2

Palpate systematically in a logical sequence from medial to lateral. Identify the exact site of point tenderness — this directs the diagnosis.

  1. Sternoclavicular joint — palpate the medial clavicle where it articulates with the manubrium. Tenderness suggests SC joint pathology (sprain, arthritis, or rare dislocation).
  2. Clavicle shaft — run finger along the entire length of the clavicle. Localised tenderness with swelling suggests clavicle fracture (most common site: junction of middle and outer thirds).
  3. Acromioclavicular joint — palpate the distal clavicle at the AC joint. This joint may be tender at baseline in many patients; compare with the contralateral side. Point tenderness here + cross-arm test positive → AC joint pathology.
  4. Acromion and coracoid process — the acromion is the posterolateral bony point of the shoulder. The coracoid is palpated 2 cm below the clavicle in the deltopectoral groove (often tender in frozen shoulder and impingement).
  5. Greater tuberosity — insertion site of supraspinatus, infraspinatus, and teres minor. Tenderness here strongly suggests rotator cuff pathology.
  6. Bicipital groove & tendon — with the patient's arm at 90° abduction and externally rotated, palpate the anterior humeral head to feel the long head of biceps tendon running in its groove. Tenderness = bicipital tendinopathy. As the arm is passively internally/externally rotated, the tendon can be felt rolling under the examiner's finger.
🩺 Clinical Correlation
The AC joint is a common source of shoulder pain in overhead athletes (e.g., weightlifters, swimmers). Isolated AC joint tenderness on palpation, combined with a positive cross-arm adduction test, confirms AC joint involvement rather than glenohumeral or rotator cuff pathology.

🔄 3. Range of Motion ▼
Step 3

Test active ROM first. If active ROM is normal, passive ROM adds little further information. Document all movements in degrees and compare bilaterally.

Forward FlexionNormal: 150–170°
ExtensionNormal: 50–70°
AbductionNormal: 150–180°
External RotationNormal: ~90°
Internal RotationNormal: T7 spinous process

Internal Rotation Measurement

Ask the patient to place their hand behind their back with the palm facing outwards. Measure the highest vertebral spinous process the thumb can reach. Normal is above T7. The dominant hand typically reaches 2 spinous processes lower than the non-dominant hand — compare sides.

Interpreting ROM Findings

ROM PatternLikely Diagnosis
Normal active ROM in all planesRules out: humeral head fracture, GH dislocation, adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder)
Globally restricted active AND passive ROMAdhesive capsulitis, severe GH arthritis, post-traumatic stiffness
Normal passive ROM; impaired active ROM (pain-limited)Rotator cuff tear (partial or full-thickness), subacromial bursitis, tendinopathy
Painful arc (60°–120° abduction)Subacromial impingement, supraspinatus tendinopathy
Painful arc (160°–180° abduction)AC joint pathology
🩺 Clinical Correlation — Adhesive Capsulitis
Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder) classically presents with progressive loss of active AND passive ROM in all planes, with external rotation being the first and most severely affected movement. It is associated with diabetes mellitus, hypothyroidism, and prolonged immobilisation.

💪 4. Muscle Strength Testing ▼
Step 4

Test the four rotator cuff muscles individually. Grade strength using the Medical Research Council (MRC) scale (0–5). Always compare sides.

📌 Memory Aid — SITS Insertions
Supraspinatus, Teres minor, Infraspinatus → insert on the posterior surface of the humerus (greater tuberosity).
Subscapularis → inserts on the anterior surface (lesser tuberosity).
Remembering this explains their actions: posterior insertions = external rotation / abduction; anterior insertion = internal rotation.

Supraspinatus — Empty Beer Can Test

Supraspinatus
Abduction (with deltoid)
  1. Position the arm at 90° abduction in the scapular plane (30° forward flexion from the coronal plane)
  2. Internally rotate the arm fully so the thumb points downward ("empty beer can" position)
  3. Patient pushes arm upward against the examiner's downward resistance
  4. Positive test: weakness or pain suggests supraspinatus tear or tendinopathy
🎯 Exam Insight
The scapular plane position (30° anterior to coronal plane) maximises supraspinatus isolation and is the most pain-free position for testing. Sensitivity ~69%, specificity ~66% for full-thickness tears.

Infraspinatus & Teres Minor — External Rotation

Infraspinatus / Teres Minor
External Rotation
  1. Patient stands or sits with elbow at 90° flexion, arm adducted to the side
  2. Patient attempts to externally rotate (push forearm outward) against examiner's resistance
  3. Positive test: weakness indicates infraspinatus tear (infraspinatus is the most commonly tested; teres minor contributes ~45% of external rotation strength)

Subscapularis — Internal Rotation / Lift-Off Test

Subscapularis
Internal Rotation
  1. Patient stands with arm at the side, elbow at 90°
  2. Resist internal rotation (patient pushes forearm inward against resistance)
  3. Alternatively: Lift-off test (Gerber's) — patient places the dorsum of the hand against the lumbar spine and attempts to lift it away; inability to do so indicates subscapularis tear

🔬 5. Special Tests ▼
Step 5

Special tests are performed after the standard exam to confirm specific diagnoses. A positive result is pain reproduction (not just discomfort) in the relevant area.

AC Joint Pathology

Cross-Arm (Adduction) Test

Tests for: AC joint injury / degeneration

  1. Patient's arm forward-flexed to 90°
  2. Examiner passively adducts arm horizontally across the body, compressing the AC joint
  3. Positive: pain specifically at the AC joint (not diffuse shoulder pain)
🎯 Exam Insight
Distinguish localised AC joint pain from general shoulder pain. Sensitivity ~77%, specificity ~79%. The most specific test for AC joint pathology when combined with direct tenderness on palpation.

Impingement Tests (Subacromial)

Both tests reproduce impingement by mechanically narrowing the subacromial space, pinching the supraspinatus tendon and subacromial bursa between the greater tuberosity and the coracoacromial arch.

Neer's Test
  1. Stabilise the scapula with one hand on the posterior shoulder to prevent scapular rotation
  2. Passively forward flex the arm (internally rotated, thumb down) with the elbow extended
  3. Positive: pain in the anterior or anterolateral shoulder at end range (>120°)
🎯 Mechanism
Forward flexion drives the greater tuberosity under the anterior acromion, impinging the supraspinatus tendon. Sensitivity ~72%, specificity ~60%.
Hawkins-Kennedy Test
  1. Arm at 90° forward flexion, elbow at 90° flexion
  2. Position arm at 30° of horizontal adduction (across the body slightly)
  3. Rapidly and forcefully internally rotate the shoulder (take from external → internal rotation)
  4. Positive: pain reproduced in the anterior shoulder region
🎯 Mechanism
Internal rotation in this position drives the greater tuberosity under the coracoacromial ligament, impinging the supraspinatus against it. Sensitivity ~79%, specificity ~59%. Generally more sensitive than Neer's test.

Labral Pathology

O'Brien's Test (Active Compression Test)

Tests for: SLAP (superior labrum anterior to posterior) tears; AC joint pathology

  1. Arm at 90° forward flexion, elbow fully extended, adducted 10–15° across midline
  2. Phase 1 — arm internally rotated (thumb pointing down): patient resists downward force by examiner
  3. Phase 2 — arm externally rotated (thumb pointing up / supinated): patient resists same downward force
  4. Positive for SLAP tear: pain or clicking with phase 1 (thumb down) that decreases or disappears with phase 2 (thumb up), and pain is felt deep in the shoulder
  5. Positive for AC pathology: pain localised to the top of the shoulder (at AC joint) in both phases
🩺 Clinical Correlation
SLAP tears are common in overhead throwing athletes (cricketers, javelin throwers, swimmers). They often present with a clicking or catching sensation with overhead activities. MRI arthrogram is the gold standard for diagnosis.

Biceps Pathology

Speed's Test

Tests for: Long head of biceps tendinopathy / SLAP tear

  1. Grasp the patient's hand in a handshake grip
  2. Resist the patient's attempt to supinate the forearm against resistance (alternatively: resisted forward flexion with elbow extended and forearm supinated)
  3. Positive: pain in the bicipital groove (anterior shoulder, along the long head of biceps tendon)

Shoulder Instability

Apprehension Test (Crank Test)

Tests for: Anterior glenohumeral instability (history of recurrent dislocations)

  1. Patient supine, arm abducted to 90°, elbow flexed to 90°
  2. Slowly externally rotate the shoulder toward 90°
  3. Positive: patient reports apprehension (feeling that the shoulder will dislocate) — not just pain
  4. Relocation test (Jobe): apply a posterior force to the humeral head — this should reduce apprehension (confirms anterior instability)
🎯 Exam Insight
The positive finding is apprehension (a feeling of impending dislocation), not just pain. Pain alone is less specific. Sensitivity ~69%, specificity ~66%. The relocation test increases specificity to ~87%.
